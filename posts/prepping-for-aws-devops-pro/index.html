How to Prep for the AWS DevOps Professional Certification in 2020

Another AWS Certification?

After passing both the AWS Certified Solutions Architect associate exam, as well as the Certified Kubernetes Application Developer (CKAD) exam, I thought I was done with certification testing for awhile. Then, as we all know, the COVID-19 global pandemic happened, and I found myself with a bit more time at home to use for self-improvement and career development. After reading quite a few books, I felt the urge to pursue another certification, and the AWS DevOps pro was the next logical step - very pertinent to my current occupational workload at the moment.

I’m going to assume the reader has a fair bit of background on AWS and this particular certification, so I won’t go too in depth about what it is or why the industry finds it valuable. However, if you would like a more introductory post on the subject, please let me know, and I can do a follow-up piece on that.

The following are my recommended study materials, strategy, and gameplan near test-day for doing well on that exam in 2020. I hope you find it helpful.

If you are interested in my other post about passing the CKAD exam, you can find my tips on preparing for that exam here.

The Exam Prep Outline

  1. Watch video training material, specifically geared for passing the exam and teaching you the exam concepts in depth
  2. Replicate all labs (within cost/reason) that I found confusing or needed additional study on in my own AWS account
  3. Switch gears and drill practice test material HARD from multiple, highly rated sources
  4. Cover any remaining knowledge gaps, using materials from recommended AWS whitepapers and blog posts
  5. Optimize your test taking experience and logistics for maximal effectiveness
  6. Ancillary Mental Strategies

Phase 1: Watch Foundational Video Training Material

You could refer to the AWS official exam guide, which lays out a high level list of topics to study, and create your own study guide, but I prefer to “stand on the shoulders of giants” and watch targetted and current hands-on training material from an instructor who has already passed the exam I’m hoping to take. In this regard, I cannot recommend Stephane Maarek and his Udemy training material enough. I actually used Stephane’s preperation course for the AWS SolArch Associate exam in 2019, and his 2020 DevOps course did NOT disappoint. Not only was every lesson extremely hands-on, but based on my experience taking the actual exam, as well as how frequently he keeps the material updated, his material was the most comprehensive and helpful for this particular exam. There were almost no lessons covered in that entire Udemy course that were not referenced in some way, shape, or form, on the actual exam. I’ve provided the link to the course below; if you could choose only one online training course to learn the topics for this exam, it should be this one.

Not only do I categorically recommend Stephane’s 2020 lecture course for this certification, but I also leveraged his companion practice test on Udemy (which I cover in Phase 3 further down in this post).

After completing the entire lecture series by Stephane mentioned above, I came across another supplementary lecture series for the DevOps certification exam, by Zeal Vora. This particular course provided both lecture material, as well as sample quizzes - all in the same Udemy course purchase. However, Zeal’s presentation style and overall explanation of the technical concepts was much less thorough and useful to me… I only watched the bits and pieces of this lecture series that I needed extra repetition on conceptually, as well as to provide a second source of information, to compare and contrast the two content authors’ material for inaccuracies. Most of the value from this lecture series came from the included practice exams, but I’ve also included a link to it below.

One final word of warning - do not use the current LinuxAcademy preparation for this particular exam. I usually love their certification prep courses, but in this case, it was horribly out of date, and after going through over 20 hours of the material, I benchmarked the knowledge against a sample test, only to realize it was woefully insufficient. Do not waste your time and money on LinuxAcademy for this particular exam.

Phase 2: Collect Feedback and Practice Labs in Real AWS Account

I intentionally chose the study material above and placed priority on Stephan Maarek’s content as he goes through almost every concept with a hands-on lab. Because learning in general is so intricately tied to deliberate practice, I made my own AWS free-tier eligible account and tried as much as possible to go through the hands-on walkthroughs in the course content. But unlike a typical hands-on follow-along, my take is that the learning actually is more effective when you doing the work without immediate guidance. I believe this reinforces your own learning better than watching someone go through it and tell you what to do as you do it, because you form the connections to how things work yourself.

Therefore, my recommendation here is to identify the labs you want to replicate yourself, either because the concepts are very important, very complex, or just still very confusing to you. Watch the hands-on lecture and the demo on what needs to be done to accomplish the task/concept being taught, then turn the video off. Log into your own AWS account, and try to replicate the lab from memory, but change some details about it, so it becomes a unique exercise. For example, the hands-on lab shows you how to create a serverless function in NodeJS which spits out “Hello, World!”, try making a serverless function which uses a different programming language, and instead of printing, pulls some instance data from an EC2 instance (as an example). The point being, you will most likely get stuck and need to refer back to the video for help - and here is the important bit: that struggle is important, and is what builds long term memory and learning.

Phase 3: Practice, Practice, Practice on Practice Tests

Once you feel like you have a good mental framework about the concepts taught in the training material you chose, it’s time to shift gears into drilling practice tests. Whereas the video lecture material will teach you the fundamental concepts, the practice test drills will teach you to apply to concepts to the unique and verbose way the AWS exams present the actual exam questions. This is equally as important as the actual knowledge of the concepts, because at exam time, there is no one there to gauge your understanding in a discussion - you literally answer 75 multiple choice questions and pass/fail on the spot.

Some extremely important things to look for in your practice test material:

  • It must provide an in-depth explanation of each answer, whether you get it correct or not. Bonus points are if it provides a direct link to AWS documentation or media which covers the question topic being referenced
  • The questions should always be randomized on each exam attempt, including the question order and the answer choice order. This is to prevent your mind from shutting off and memorizing the answer choices, allowing you to take the same exam multiple times and continue to gain value from it
  • You should be able to pause the exam and pick up at any point, with it saving your progress. Most of the time, I only had time to drill between 10-15 questions per day, so I ended up making a 60+ question practice test take the entire work week
  • The questions should be correct - this should be obvious, but some lesser training material was either severely out of date, or used reference architectural patterns that were either too new as to be included on the exam, or just plain wrong. You DO NOT want to be drilling incorrect answers for an exam where every second counts and your mental energy will be absolutely drained 2+ hours in, to be second guessing your own answers.

There is also a specific information gathering strategy that I find important while drilling practice tests. Essentially, you want to get the wrong answers. You need to mentally accept that every answer you get wrong is more beneficial than one you get correct, because it is an indicator of what you don’t yet fully remember.

A highly effective routine while going through a practice test drill is to aim to do 10-20 questions at a time, and then stop. Grade your answers, and for each answer you got wrong, go find the documentation, and deep dive into it. Your goal should be to fully understand why the correct answer is correct, AND to ideally understand how to use that knowledge as a devops engineer.

For example, if there was a question asking something like: Should you construct a CodePipeline with multiple CodeCommit repositories for a develop, feature, and release branch of the same codebase? and you answered yes, you not only want to understand WHY it is more correct to use a single CodeCommit repository for a CICD pipeline in CodePipeline, but also a few common patterns in which those individual branches could be used to accomplish various deployment objectives, or what the CICD pipeline would have to look like if your organization decided to use Github instead of CodeCommit, etc.

In other words, don’t just memorize the right answers - aim to understand why the answers are right.

As mentioned earlier, I used both the companion DevOps pro practice exam from Stephane Maarek’s main 2020 lecture series, as well as the mini practice exams at the end of Zeal Vora’s lecture series - both of them being Udemy courses. I’ve provided the links below. You should aim to go through each practice exam between 2-3 times EACH prior to exam week.

Phase 4: Finishing Touches

Once you are consistently scoring around 90% on your practice exam drills, and you can look at any one of the high level concepts below and understand how to construct CICD pipelines or accomplish common DevOps tasks using the tools below, then you are ready to move on to the finishing touches of the exam preparation. If you aren’t reasonably sure you know what the technologies or concepts below do, then you are NOT READY for the exam.

  • CloudFormation
  • CodePipeline / CodeBuild / CodeDeploy
  • Elastic Beanstalk
  • CloudWatch
  • AWS Config
  • AWS SSM
  • Parameter Store vs. Secrets Manager
  • S3
  • Kinesis, Kinesis Streams, and Kinesis Firehose
  • Lambda
  • API Gateway
  • Deployments using Lambda and API Gateway
  • Step Functions
  • HA Architectures using EC2, RDS, Aurora, ELBs, ASGs, and multi-AZ/region deployments
  • Using the technologies above to do: Canary vs. Rolling vs. Immutable vs. Rolling w/ Additional Batch vs. In Place deployments, and their tradeoffs
  • How multi-account enterprise AWS organizations manage configurations

If the above concepts feel good, then it’s time to soak up any last little bits of information in the AWS DevOps Blog, and the three key DevOps whitepapers.

Phase 5: Test Week Game Plan

This probably should have been stated earlier, but when choosing your time and date for your exam, it is imperitive in my opinion to choose a time and date that works best for you and your body. What do I mean by this? Lets say you are a morning person - you are the most mentally clear right when you wake up. You should schedule your exam on a Monday, the earliest you possibly can after waking up and doing your morning routine (I chose Monday at 7:30 AM, because I am an early riser). Similarly, if you have commitments throughout the day with family or work, try to schedule it to maximize the amount of mental energy and minimize the amount of stress prior to sitting. This means you may have to take off some time from work. You will not be able to focus on this exam if you have to work the entire day first.

In the current pandemic situation, scheduling and logistics is even more important. In my case, I chose to take the test at home, to avoid being in a testing center and risking exposure. If you choose to go this route, then you have additional concerns of making sure your work area to take the test meets the testing criteria. Be absolutely sure that it does well ahead of time. AWS will let you cancel your exam and refund your money, or let you reschedule for later, as long as you do it early enough. Lets say one week before your exam, you take the system compatibility test and realize the testing client doesn’t work with your computer. No harm - just cancel and move it out after you figure it out. But once you are within 24 hours of testing time, all bets are off - the last thing you want to be stressing about is that your workstation or laptop will not run the client, that your webcam doesn’t work, that your desk isn’t free of distractions, or that you won’t have a 3+ hour windows of undisturbed time, etc.

Exam Day

Okay, now you’ve studied with what remaining time you have left, and it’s exam day! Here are a few things I did which I feel helped contribute to me passing the exam on test day. Feel free to pick and choose the ones that make sense for you:

  1. I did a strenuous workout which released endorphins about 5 hours prior to the exam. I allowed myself to physically recover, and had a post-workout meal and coffee to start the morning. This put my mind in perfect balance, and prevented me from being restless during the exam (it’s a lot of sitting still, which can be challenging for people who like to fidget)
  2. I had my coffee first, but then gave myself at least 2 hours before the exam to go to the bathroom and avoid liquids, to minimize the chances of having to pee midway through. They will disqualify you if you get up for any reason (including a bathroom break)
  3. I cleaned off my entire workstation the night before to minimize the chances that the proctor watching me would hassle me over some innocuous stapler or notepad sitting nearby. I tend to get anxiety if my mental preparation is jostled by an unexpected haruanging from a faceless proctor.
  4. I intentionally did not study the day before test day, and instead allowed my mind to be blank, and relax. This is akin to how powerlifters will take an entire day off prior to the competition, to allow for supercompensation. I’m very sure this works for the mind. If you’ve been studying dilligently, and spread it out over a sufficient length of time, taking the day off before exam day will do wonders for your concentration.
  5. During the exam, I made sure to take deep belly breaths every 5 questions or so. This kept my pacing is good and prevented me from excess eye strain.
  6. I answered the questions in the exam formulaically, using the strategy I outline below under the Multiple Choice Format Test Taking Protocol

Phase 6: Ancillary Strategies

Here are some other important strategies I employed to prepare for the exam

Mental Visualization of DevOps Architectures

When you were going through practice test drills, you were bound to come across large questions which involved a lot of AWS technologies describing a complicated architecture. Quickly visualizing these reference architectures and CodePipeline configurations in your mind’s eye will also serve you well during the exam. Try to imagine the symbols that describe where each technology fits into the architecture, and associated funny words with how the relationships work together. This is an ultralearning mnemonic strategy that world-class memory champions use, and there is no reason you can’t use it too. For more info on this in general, I highly recommend reading “Moonwalking With Einstein”.

Multiple Choice Format Test Taking Protocol

After taking the AWS Solutions Architect exam and passing it, as well as reading a ton of material on the subject, and talking with my friend and fellow expert exam taker Wes Etheredge, I’ve settled on a certification exam protocol that works well for long-winded, complicated, multiple-choice format tests.

The protocol is executed in three passes, and uses a specific method to answer the questions.

The Method

The optimal, formulaic method to approach each AWS exam question can be summarized simply:

  1. scan
  2. eliminate
  3. deep read
  4. answer or guess

scan - First, you will want to scan the question quickly, focusing on the key terms, technologies, and architectural patterns or constraints. Notice I said scan - you will only scan because the primary thing you will do after scanning is to focus on the actual answer choices. Just get enough info to paint a rough mental image, and move on to the eliminate step.

eliminate - In these exams, there are usually between 1-2 answer choices that are either technically incorrect, or correct, but have no relevance to the situation being described in the question. That being said, you should aim to eliminate these obviously wrong choices first, which both increases your % chance of selecting the right answer if you have to guess, as well as lowering the total number of possible choices when you go back to deep read the question.

deep read - After potentially eliminating some bad answer choices and increasing your odds of success, you will now go back to the question and deep read it. Now, you are aiming to really understand EXACTLY what it is asking you. Pay attention to little gotchas in the question here, like optimizing for LOWEST cost or the RPO must be 3 hours or less. Stuff that could change the answer entirely but that you could miss if you weren’t really paying attention.

answer or guess - Finally, select the answer. If at this point, you still have no idea, just guess. You have already increased your odds of guessing correctly from the earlier steps, and by taking the time to have done the previous steps, you may actually help yourself answer a question later on in the exam with the analysis done on this particular question. NOTE - pay special attention to the types of questions that ask you to select multiple answer choices. If your earlier steps eliminated some incorrect choices, and your remaining pool of multiple-answer checkboxes no longer make coherent sense (meaning, if you were to select whatever remains, and it still doesn’t answer the question correctly), then go back to the eliminate step and rethink what you chose to eliminate. In almost EVERY question which asks you to choose between 2-3 correct answers, all of the choices you select will be part of a total solution, so they will all need to logically fit together. It’s rarely the case that the three correct answer choices are unrelated to each other.

The passes

  1. First Pass - your goal, starting at question 1, is to answer as many questions as you can up until the last question of the exam, only answering the ones which you feel reasonably confident or better that you know the answer to. Flag every other question for review that you don’t feel like you are sure you know the answer to, and skip it. You will return to these on the second pass. By reasonably confident, I mean on a scale from 1-10, with 1 being I have never seen this question or set of answers in my life, and 10 being Are you joking? Too easy!, you should answer questions here which fall between 7-10. The idea is to save as much time as possible for the second and third pass, and get as many easy points as possible while wasting as little time as possible. In all probability, this pass may take you about 1 hour.
  2. Second Pass - the second pass, which you may also spend about an hour on, is solely focused on re-examining the questions you flagged for review in the first pass. By saving these harder questions for last, you may find that they are slightly easier to answer, as you may have been exposed to some helpful information in the question body of an exam problem later on in the test. You will want to deep dive into your mind, recall intricate and arcane tidbits of knowledge, and liberally use the online notepad to diagram out these problems, if it helps. Your goal here should be to place an answer on every remaining question that you skipped earlier.
  3. Final Pass - the last pass, should you have any time left in the exam, is reserved for doing one final quick skim through of your answer choices, starting from question 1, to the end. At this point, your mental energy is probably almost completely gone, you’re tired, and your brain is most likely melting. That’s normal. You are now in no place to second guess the answers you selected in the first two passes - the only point to this last pass is to ensure you answered every question and to look briefly once more at the questions where your confidence level was between 1-3. Since questions answered with such a low confidence level were most likely guesses, you are giving yourself one final time to confirm your guesses, and use whatever remaining mental reserves you have to ensure those guesses still make sense.

Other Miscellaneous Musings

  • This certification tests you heavily on CICD patterns about GitFlow, but AWS themselves recommend trunk-based development … I expect soon their exam questions will change to reflect this over time.
  • I spent over 3 months preparing for this exam, spending on average 1.5 hours per day studying or test question prepping, Monday through Friday. Could I have done it faster? Of course, but I care more about knowledge retention, which science shows is maximized by spaced repetition.
  • Almost all of the questions should be approached from the perspective of How could I answer this if I could only use the AWS ecosystem?. What I mean is - these AWS exams are designed to certify you for their products. If you are reading a question where one of the answer choices makes sense to you, but using a third party tool, instead of a native AWS offering, which does the same thing … you should REALLY think hard about why your answer is more correct. Because 95% of the time, the native AWS offering is correct.
  • You should really understand what a canary and blue/green deployment are. And don’t get blue/green confused with immutable. Immutable = only one load balancer.
  • The exam is 300 USD, but if you’ve already passed a previous AWS certification, you can get it for half price by using a built-in discount code that you can view once you are logged into your certification account.
  • I actually think the AWS Solutions Architect Professional exam (based on the training I’ve taken for it thus far), is way more difficult than this, because it’s highly unlikely that you could have touched all of the services that the SolArch pro asks you about, whereas the DevOps pro has a TON of overlap with common CICD work that you may have already done as a DevOps engineer at your day job - it’s mostly a matter of translating those concepts into the AWS-way of doing them.
  • The best way I personally know I’m ready for a certification exam is when I just want to get the exam over with, whether I pass it or fail it. Usually, if I’m honest with myself, and I no longer care whether I pass or fail, I know I’ve studied enough to have gained the knowledge, which will be useful to me with or without a piece of paper telling others I know it. Maybe this internal benchmark can be helpful to gauge your own readiness?

Wrapping Up

I hope this brain dump helps you succeed. I also encourage you to read as many other articles, blog posts, and write-ups from people who have passed it. Trust me when I tell you that you will be mentally and physically exhausted after taking this exam. You will need all of the ammuniction you can get, so leverage only the best tips, and dump the rest.
